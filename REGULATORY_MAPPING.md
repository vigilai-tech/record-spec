# RECORD — Regulatory Mapping

This document maps every RECORD schema field to the specific regulatory obligations that motivate its capture. Compliance teams should use this table to demonstrate that a RECORD-instrumented system satisfies the evidence-preservation requirements of each cited framework.

> **Frameworks covered:** EU AI Act (2024/1689) · MiFID II RTS 6 · FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 · Federal Reserve SR 11-7 · ECOA Regulation B §202.9 · SOX §302

---

## Field-by-Field Regulatory Mapping

| Field | Regulatory Obligation | Article / Section | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| `record_id` | Unique identification of every AI-system output record | EU AI Act Art. 12(1); SOX §302 | Regulators and auditors must be able to retrieve and correlate a specific decision trace. A stable UUID enables point-in-time reconstruction and links the record to downstream artefacts (tickets, filings, trade cancellations). SOX §302 certifications require that individual records be traceable to the signing officer's representation of accuracy. |
| `timestamp` | Timestamped logging of AI system outputs | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(a); MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Regulators require that logs record *when* a decision was made to establish sequencing, detect latency-induced errors, and correlate with market data snapshots. MiFID II RTS 6 mandates timestamp granularity sufficient to reconstruct intraday order-flow context. |
| `agent_id` | Identification of the AI system responsible for a decision | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(a); SR 11-7 §Model Inventory; SOX §302 | Model-risk governance requires that every decision be attributable to a specific, inventoried model. EU AI Act Art. 13 requires high-risk AI systems to disclose their identity to deployers. SOX requires that internal controls identify which system produced a material output. |
| `agent_version` | Version control and change management for AI models | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(c); SR 11-7 §Change Management; MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(2) | Version pinning enables regulators to reproduce the exact model behaviour at a past decision point. SR 11-7 requires that model changes be documented and validated before deployment. MiFID II RTS 6 requires that algorithmic trading systems log the version of logic applied to each order. |
| `authority_basis` | Explicit linkage of every decision to its governing regulatory framework | EU AI Act Art. 86(1); FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.230; ECOA Reg B §202.9 | This field provides the machine-readable audit-chain connecting a decision to the specific obligation that required it to be made. EU AI Act Art. 86 creates a right of explanation that can only be satisfied if the system can identify which regulatory mandate drove its logic. FinCEN's proposed AML rules require that AI-assisted SAR decisions reference the specific AML programme requirement they address. ECOA §202.9 requires that adverse-action notices identify the legal basis for credit decisions. |
| `operating_domain` | Declaration of human oversight level | EU AI Act Art. 14 (Human Oversight); Art. 26(5); SR 11-7 §Use and Ongoing Monitoring | EU AI Act Art. 14 mandates that high-risk AI systems be designed to allow effective human oversight; deployers must document the level of oversight exercised. SR 11-7 requires firms to disclose whether model outputs are used autonomously or subject to expert override. This field captures the operating mode as a machine-readable enum for automated governance dashboards. |
| `influencing_parameters[].factor` | Feature-level explainability for high-risk decisions | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(d) (transparency); ECOA Reg B §202.9(b)(2) (specific reasons); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Regulators require identification of the principal factors driving an AI decision. ECOA §202.9(b)(2) mandates that adverse-action notices list the specific reasons for denial — generic outputs like "model score" do not satisfy this requirement. SR 11-7 requires that model documentation describe input variables and their relative importance. |
| `influencing_parameters[].value` | Data-point capture at decision time | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Data Quality; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.210 | The value of each feature at decision time must be preserved to allow post-hoc review and challenge. SR 11-7 requires evidence that model inputs met data-quality standards. FinCEN's proposed rules require that AI AML systems retain the transaction attributes that triggered a suspicious-activity disposition. |
| `influencing_parameters[].weight` | Feature importance / explainability weights | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(d); ECOA Reg B §202.9(b)(2); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Quantified feature weights allow reviewers to assess whether the model's reasoning was proportionate and non-discriminatory. ECOA requires that the most significant factors be disclosed on adverse-action notices; weights operationalise "most significant." |
| `policy_evaluation[].rule_id` | Identification of each policy rule applied | MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1)(d); EU AI Act Art. 12(1); SR 11-7 §Governance | Unique rule IDs provide the audit-chain linking a verdict to the exact policy artefact in the firm's rule library. MiFID II RTS 6 requires that algorithmic systems document which pre-trade controls were applied to each order. |
| `policy_evaluation[].rule_name` | Human-readable policy rule description | EU AI Act Art. 13(1) (transparency); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Rule names allow non-technical reviewers (compliance officers, regulators) to understand which policies were evaluated without consulting internal code. EU AI Act Art. 13 requires that AI system outputs be interpretable by the deployer's oversight personnel. |
| `policy_evaluation[].verdict` | Pass/fail outcome of each policy rule | MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1)(d); EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SOX §302 | The verdict record proves that each required policy check was actually executed and not bypassed. MiFID II RTS 6 requires that pre-trade risk controls produce a logged outcome for every order. SOX requires that internal controls over material processes demonstrate effectiveness through evidence. |
| `policy_evaluation[].confidence` | Agent confidence in each policy verdict | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(d); SR 11-7 §Model Uncertainty | Regulators increasingly require that AI systems communicate their uncertainty. SR 11-7 requires model documentation to address model uncertainty and limitations. Low-confidence verdicts may require escalation under firm policy, and this field operationalises that threshold. |
| `decision_lineage.parent_trace_id` | Traceability to upstream system outputs | EU AI Act Art. 12(1); SOX §302; SR 11-7 §Audit Trail | A complete audit chain requires that multi-step agent pipelines be traversable from final decision back to originating alert. Parent trace linkage enables regulators to reconstruct the full decision path across microservices and agent handoffs. |
| `decision_lineage.case_id` | Linkage to case management system | MiFID II RTS 6; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.320; SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring | Case IDs connect the RECORD trace to the operational workflow (alert, SAR, credit application) that the agent was acting on. FinCEN requires that SAR filings reference the case identifiers used in the firm's AML monitoring system. |
| `decision_lineage.investigation_thread_id` | Multi-step investigation grouping | FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.320; EU AI Act Art. 12(1); SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring | Complex financial-crime investigations may span dozens of AI-assisted steps. The thread ID groups related RECORDs so that reviewers can reconstruct the full analytical narrative, as required by FinCEN for SAR documentation. |
| `data_at_decision[].source` | Provenance of data used in the decision | EU AI Act Art. 10(3) (data governance); SR 11-7 §Data Quality; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.210 | Regulators require that AI systems be able to demonstrate the quality and lineage of their input data. Identifying the source allows reviewers to assess whether data met the firm's data-quality standards at decision time. |
| `data_at_decision[].query` | Exact query sent to each data source | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation; SOX §302 | The query record enables reproducibility: a reviewer can re-run the identical query against a point-in-time data snapshot to verify that the agent's inputs were accurate. SOX §302 requires that material financial decisions be reproducible and verifiable. |
| `data_at_decision[].payload_hash` | Cryptographic integrity proof of data at decision | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SOX §302; MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1) | A SHA-256 hash of the raw data payload provides tamper-evidence: any post-hoc modification to the data record will be detectable. SOX and MiFID II require that audit records not be alterable after the fact. |
| `data_at_decision[].payload_preview` | Human-readable data excerpt for reviewer comprehension | EU AI Act Art. 13(1); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Compliance reviewers and regulators often lack the tooling to reconstruct hashed payloads; a preview allows them to understand what data the agent saw without requiring raw data access. EU AI Act Art. 13 requires that AI system operation be comprehensible to deployer oversight personnel. |
| `human_disposition.action` | Record of human override, confirmation, or abstention | EU AI Act Art. 14(4) (human oversight); SR 11-7 §Use and Ongoing Monitoring; ECOA Reg B §202.9 | EU AI Act Art. 14(4) requires deployers to ensure that human oversight personnel can intervene in and override AI outputs. This field records whether they did so, creating an evidence record that the oversight right was exercised (or deliberately waived by abstention). ECOA requires that the final credit decision — not just the model output — be documented. |
| `human_disposition.rationale` | Human analyst's justification for their action | EU AI Act Art. 14(4); ECOA Reg B §202.9(b)(2); SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring | A free-text rationale documents *why* a human overrode or confirmed the agent, enabling retrospective review of whether override decisions were principled. ECOA requires that adverse-action reasons reflect the *actual* basis for the decision, including any human override of a model score. |
| `human_disposition.analyst_id` | Pseudonymous identifier of the reviewing analyst | SOX §302; EU AI Act Art. 26(5); SR 11-7 §Governance | SOX §302 requires that certifying officers be able to identify which employee approved or reviewed a material decision. EU AI Act Art. 26(5) requires deployers to assign oversight responsibilities to named personnel. Pseudonymisation satisfies GDPR constraints while preserving accountability. |
| `human_disposition.reviewed_at` | Timestamp of human review | MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1); SOX §302; SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring | The review timestamp establishes the sequence between agent recommendation and human decision, critical for demonstrating that humans reviewed outputs *before* consequential actions were taken (e.g., before a SAR was filed or a trade was cancelled). |
| `outcome_linkage.type` | Category of downstream artefact | FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.320; MiFID II RTS 6; SOX §302 | Classifying the downstream artefact type allows automated governance systems to verify that every high-risk AI decision resulted in an appropriate action (e.g., that every escalated AML alert produced either a SAR filing or a documented no-file decision). |
| `outcome_linkage.reference_id` | Identifier of the downstream artefact | FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.320; SOX §302; EU AI Act Art. 12(1) | The reference ID links the RECORD trace to the operational record of what actually happened (the filed SAR, the cancelled trade, the adjusted credit limit). This closed-loop linkage is required by FinCEN to demonstrate that AML monitoring produces actionable outcomes. |
| `outcome_linkage.status` | Current status of the downstream artefact | EU AI Act Art. 12(1); SOX §302; SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring | Status tracking allows governance teams to detect stalled or unresolved cases, which may indicate control failures. SOX §302 requires that material internal control weaknesses be identified and disclosed. |
| `prompt` | Full input instruction to the agent | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation; SOX §302 | The verbatim prompt is the single most important reproducibility artefact: it allows regulators to re-run or audit the exact instructions given to the model. EU AI Act Art. 12 requires that inputs to high-risk AI systems be logged. SR 11-7 requires that model documentation include a description of all inputs. SOX requires that material process steps be verifiable. |
| `chain_of_thought[].content` | Intermediate reasoning steps | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(d) (interpretability); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation; ECOA Reg B §202.9(b)(2) | Chain-of-thought capture is the primary mechanism for satisfying AI interpretability requirements. EU AI Act Art. 13 requires that high-risk AI systems produce outputs that are interpretable by deployers. For ECOA adverse-action notices, chain-of-thought enables compliance teams to extract the genuine reasons for a credit decision rather than relying solely on feature weights. |
| `chain_of_thought[].type` | Semantic label for each reasoning step | EU AI Act Art. 13(1); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | Classifying reasoning steps (observation, hypothesis, analysis, conclusion) allows automated compliance tooling to verify that required analytical steps (e.g., sanctions check before SAR disposition) were performed in the correct sequence. |
| `tools_accessed[].tool_name` | Identity of each external tool or API called | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Third-Party Model Risk; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.210 | Regulators require that AI systems disclose their data-access patterns. SR 11-7 extends model-risk governance to third-party tools and data providers accessed by AI systems. FinCEN requires that AML AI systems document their data sources, including third-party sanctions and customer-risk databases. |
| `tools_accessed[].timestamp` | Timestamp of each tool call | MiFID II RTS 6 Art. 10(1); EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(a); SR 11-7 §Audit Trail | Tool-call timestamps establish the sequence of data retrieval relative to the decision, allowing reviewers to verify that data was fresh and that tools were called in the correct order (e.g., sanctions check before credit decision). |
| `tools_accessed[].query` | Exact query passed to each tool | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Data Quality; SOX §302 | The exact query enables reproducibility: a reviewer can determine precisely what the agent asked and whether the question was well-formed. Malformed queries that produced inaccurate results can be identified and attributed to a specific model version. |
| `tool_results[].payload` | Full or summarised tool response | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Data Quality; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.210 | The tool response is part of the agent's effective input at decision time. Capturing it is required to demonstrate that the agent acted on accurate, complete data. FinCEN requires that AML AI systems retain the data that informed a SAR disposition. |
| `final_action.type` | Category of the agent's ultimate decision | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(b); SR 11-7 §Use; MiFID II RTS 6; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 | A machine-readable action type enables automated compliance reporting: governance dashboards can tally escalation rates, close rates, and SAR-filing rates by agent version, enabling SR 11-7 ongoing-monitoring requirements to be met programmatically. |
| `final_action.disposition` | Free-text summary of the decision and rationale | EU AI Act Art. 13(1); ECOA Reg B §202.9(b)(2); SR 11-7 §Model Documentation | The disposition narrative is the primary human-readable audit record of the agent's decision. ECOA requires that adverse-action notices convey the genuine reasons for a credit decision in plain language. EU AI Act Art. 13 requires that AI system outputs be understandable to the deployer's oversight personnel. |
| `final_action.severity_score` | Numeric risk or severity score | SR 11-7 §Model Output Validation; MiFID II RTS 6; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 | Severity scores support tiered escalation policies (e.g., score ≥ 7 → mandatory L2 review) and enable SR 11-7 model performance monitoring by tracking score distributions over time. MiFID II requires that alert-prioritisation logic be documented and consistently applied. |
| `final_action.escalated` | Whether the case was escalated | EU AI Act Art. 14(4); FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 §1010.320; SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring; MiFID II RTS 6 | Escalation records are required to demonstrate that the human-oversight pathway functioned. EU AI Act Art. 14(4) requires deployers to document that oversight mechanisms were operative. FinCEN requires that AML programmes include escalation procedures and evidence that they were followed. |
| `final_action.escalation_reason` | Machine-readable reason for escalation | EU AI Act Art. 14(4); SR 11-7 §Ongoing Monitoring; FinCEN AML NPRM 2026 | Structured escalation reasons enable automated analysis of *why* the agent escalated, supporting SR 11-7 ongoing-monitoring requirements and allowing firms to detect systemic model weaknesses (e.g., a model that always escalates because it cannot resolve a particular pattern). |
| `openinference_span_id` | Correlation with OpenInference/OTel observability | SR 11-7 §Audit Trail; EU AI Act Art. 12(1) | Links the RECORD document to the broader observability trace, enabling joint review of compliance evidence alongside performance metrics (latency, token usage, retrieval quality) in a single investigation. |
| `model_id` | Foundation model identifier | EU AI Act Art. 13(3)(a); SR 11-7 §Model Inventory; SOX §302 | Identifies the specific foundation model (including version) whose weights produced the output. Required for SR 11-7 model inventory, EU AI Act technical documentation, and for attributing outputs to a specific model in SOX certifications. |
| `jurisdiction` | Primary regulatory jurisdiction | EU AI Act Art. 2 (scope); FinCEN AML NPRM 2026; MiFID II | Multi-jurisdictional firms must route records to the appropriate regulatory retention store. Jurisdiction tagging enables automated compliance routing and supports cross-border enforcement cooperation. |
| `schema_version` | Version of the RECORD schema | EU AI Act Art. 12(1)(c); SR 11-7 §Change Management | Schema versioning ensures that log records remain interpretable as the standard evolves, and allows compliance teams to demonstrate that records were produced under the schema version in effect at the time of the decision. |

## Regulatory Framework Summaries

### EU AI Act (2024/1689)

The EU AI Act classifies financial AI systems used for credit scoring, AML monitoring, and trading surveillance as **high-risk** under Annex III. High-risk systems must comply with:

- **Art. 10** — Data governance and data quality requirements for training and runtime data
- **Art. 12** — Automatic logging of events sufficient to ensure traceability
- **Art. 13** — Transparency obligations: outputs must be interpretable by deployers
- **Art. 14** — Human oversight: systems must be designed to allow effective human intervention
- **Art. 26** — Deployer obligations: assign oversight responsibilities, monitor operations
- **Art. 86** — Right of explanation: affected persons may request an explanation of an AI-assisted decision

### MiFID II RTS 6 (Commission Delegated Regulation 2017/589)

RTS 6 governs algorithmic trading systems, including AI-driven order-routing, market-surveillance, and pre-trade risk control systems. Article 10 requires that firms maintain records sufficient to reconstruct:

- The logic applied to each order at the time of entry
- The pre-trade risk controls evaluated and their outcomes
- The identity and version of the algorithm responsible

### FinCEN AML NPRM 2026

FinCEN's proposed rule (Bank Secrecy Act amendment, 2026 NPRM) requires covered financial institutions using AI in AML programmes to:

- Document the data sources, model logic, and decision rationale for each SAR disposition
- Maintain records that allow examiners to verify that the AI programme meets minimum programme requirements
- Retain records for the standard BSA retention period (5 years)

### Federal Reserve SR 11-7

SR 11-7 (Guidance on Model Risk Management) establishes a three-stage framework — development, validation, use — for all models that inform material decisions. Key requirements addressed by RECORD:

- **Model inventory**: every model must be catalogued with version and owner (`agent_id`, `agent_version`, `model_id`)
- **Documentation**: inputs, outputs, and logic must be documented (`prompt`, `influencing_parameters`, `chain_of_thought`)
- **Ongoing monitoring**: model performance must be tracked over time (`policy_evaluation`, `final_action.severity_score`)
- **Change management**: changes to model version must be logged (`agent_version`, `schema_version`)

### ECOA Regulation B §202.9

Section 202.9 requires that creditors provide adverse-action notices to applicants, including:

- The specific reasons for the adverse action (max four principal reasons)
- The right to request the information the creditor relied upon

RECORD's `influencing_parameters` (with weights), `chain_of_thought`, and `human_disposition.rationale` collectively provide the evidence base from which §202.9-compliant notices can be generated automatically.

### SOX §302

SOX §302 certifications require that the principal executive and financial officers certify that material internal controls are effective and that they have disclosed all known material weaknesses. AI systems that produce material financial outputs (trading decisions, credit approvals, SAR filings) must be subject to controls that are documented, tested, and auditable. RECORD provides the tamper-evident log (via `data_at_decision[].payload_hash`, `record_id`, `timestamp`) required to demonstrate control effectiveness to external auditors.
